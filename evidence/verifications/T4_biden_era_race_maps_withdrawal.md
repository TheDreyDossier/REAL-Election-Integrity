# Template Verification #4 — "DOJ Withdrew From Biden-Era Suit Demanding Race-Based Election Maps"

## DOJ's Claim (Dashboard)
- **Appears in:** 3 states — Alabama (3/14/2025), Louisiana (3/6/2025), Texas (1/24/2025)
- **Displayed as:** 3 discrete "verified enforcement actions"
- **DOJ description:** "DOJ withdrew from a Biden-era lawsuit that aimed to require Alabama to use race-based election maps. Confirming that DOJ's position is correct, the Supreme Court ruled in Louisiana v. Callais that drawing election maps based on race is unconstitutional under the Fourteenth Amendment, which guarantees equal protection of the law to all Americans."
- **Source link on DOJ page:** https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/us-department-justice-dismisses-biden-era-lawsuit-against-alabama-order-have-more-secure

## Verified Reality (CONFIRMED)

**This is DOJ withdrawing from Voting Rights Act enforcement — framed on an "enforcement" dashboard as an enforcement action.**

### What actually happened
- **In each of these three cases, the DOJ had been an original plaintiff** enforcing Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act against state redistricting maps that federal courts had found (or the DOJ argued) discriminated against Black and Latino voters.
- After Trump's inauguration in January 2025, DOJ **withdrew from these cases**, leaving private plaintiffs to continue on their own.

### Case-by-case
- **Alabama (dismissed 3/14/2025):** DOJ dismissed *United States v. Alabama*, a suit the Biden DOJ had filed in 2024 challenging Alabama's process for removing suspected noncitizens from voter rolls right before the 2024 election ([DOJ press release, 3/14/2025](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/us-department-justice-dismisses-biden-era-lawsuit-against-alabama-order-have-more-secure)). Note: this suit was **not** a redistricting case — DOJ's own framing on the current dashboard misrepresents which case was dropped. The related redistricting case in Alabama, *Allen v. Milligan*, continues without DOJ.
- **Texas (withdrew 1/24/2025 – 3/13/2025):** DOJ dropped its lawsuit alleging Texas's 2021 congressional and legislative maps discriminated against Latino and Black voters ([Texas Tribune, 3/13/2025](https://www.texastribune.org/2025/03/13/texas-redistricting-lawsuit-justice-department-withdraws/)). Private plaintiffs continued the case; on **11/18/2025 a federal court enjoined Texas from using its new 2025 map** as a likely racial gerrymander ([CNN, 11/18/2025](https://www.cnn.com/2025/11/18/politics/texas-redistricting-trump-court-ruling)).
- **Louisiana (withdrew 1/24/2025):** DOJ withdrew its amicus brief in *Louisiana v. Callais* — the brief that had defended Louisiana's second majority-Black district. Four days after Trump's inauguration, the Acting Solicitor General notified the Supreme Court that "the previously filed brief no longer represents the position of the United States" ([DOJ CRT letter](https://www.justice.gov/crt/media/1388361/dl)). Then in **September 2025, DOJ filed a new amicus arguing Section 2 is unconstitutional as applied** ([Democracy Docket, 9/25/2025](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/once-the-voting-rights-acts-champion-doj-now-wants-scotus-to-gut-it/)).

### The broader pattern (BRENNAN CENTER, June 2025)
- **DOJ has withdrawn from at least seven Section 2 voting rights cases** since Trump's inauguration ([Brennan Center analysis](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/justice-department-shirking-its-responsibility-voters))
- Cases dropped or withdrawn from include Alabama, Texas, Louisiana, Georgia, Virginia, and Houston County (GA)
- Georgia: DOJ dismissed its own lawsuit over the state's 2021 election law restricting Black voter access ([WYPR, 3/24/2025](https://www.wypr.org/2025-03-24/under-trump-the-justice-department-is-stepping-away-from-some-voting-rights-cases))

### The Supreme Court context
- The dashboard's own description invokes *Louisiana v. Callais* as vindication. The Supreme Court decided *Callais* on **April 29, 2026** in a 6-3 opinion by Alito ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_v._Callais); [SCOTUSblog](https://preview.scotusblog.com/cases/louisiana-v-callais-2/)).
- **The Court did NOT hold that "drawing election maps based on race is unconstitutional" as a general matter**, as the DOJ dashboard claims. The Court held Louisiana's specific 2024 map was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander, and "did not hold Section 2 of the VRA to be unconstitutional, nor did it overturn cases such as *Allen v. Milligan* (2023)" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_v._Callais)).
- Justice Kagan's dissent, quoted in Wikipedia: "The consequences [of the Court's decision] are likely to be far-reaching and grave. Today's decision renders Section 2 all but a dead letter."
- Harvard Kennedy School: The ruling "significantly weakened the 1965 Voting Rights Act" and "severely limited" Section 2.
- Brennan Center: "The ruling in Louisiana v. Callais is the latest in the Court's decades-long project to destroy the Voting Rights Act. Its effect will be to allow racial discrimination and further encourage partisan gerrymandering."

## Structural Assessment

**REFRAMING INVERSION → CATEGORY ERROR**

Three ways the dashboard's framing is wrong:

1. **The framing inverts what enforcement means.** Withdrawing from a lawsuit that sought to remedy racial vote dilution is *un-enforcement* of the Voting Rights Act. The dashboard presents this as an "election integrity enforcement action."

2. **The description mischaracterizes what Callais held.** The Court did not rule that "drawing election maps based on race is unconstitutional" as a categorical matter. It ruled that Louisiana's specific map failed strict scrutiny. It left Section 2 formally intact but functionally gutted.

3. **The 2025 Alabama dismissal was not about redistricting.** The Alabama press release DOJ links to concerns *voter roll purges*, not maps. Yet the dashboard entry claims it was a "Race-Based Election Maps" case. This is a factual error in DOJ's own text: their linked source contradicts their own summary.
